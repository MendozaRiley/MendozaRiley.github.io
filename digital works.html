What's New Media Art?

Guest Artists
  • Carla Gannis
  • Daniel Temkin
  • LaTurbo Avedon

New Media Art involves the search for and experimentation with novel and different ways of making art. These practices can include anything from virtual to conceptual art. For instance, Carla Gannis, Daniel Temkin, and LaTurbo Avedon represent New Media Art in different ways through their work.

Carla Gannis is an American transmedia artist who works from Brooklyn, New York. Gannis graduated with an MFA in painting from Boston University and is currently the Assistant Chair of the Department of Digital Arts at Pratt Institute. Gannis began to blend digital elements and styles in with her mainly painting-based work in the late 1990s. Her work then developed into the remixes and collages which showcase alienation and democratization in technology that she is known for today.

Through her work, Gannis explores the nature of identity as a compound where the real and virtual versions of the self intertwine. While Gannis breaks down stiff barriers of perception and reality, her viewers are able to realize how mediated contemporary life is through the digital lens. Gannis’ 52 digital drawing series “The Selfie Drawings” was published in a public online space. The series was a year long project from 2015 to 2016 where Gannis illustrated “the self.” The project involved a combination of modern selfie photography and traditional self portraits. In the series, Gannis explores her personal and public identity hidden from and revealed in the digital world.



Carla Gannis' Selfie Drawing 01 and Selfie Drawing 52 "Powers of Ten"

Similarly to Carla Gannis, I want to explore identity within my own artwork with relation to who people are and how they want to be and are perceived by others.

Daniel Temkin creates images, interactive works, and programming languages while exploring different systems of language and logic. In 2012, Temkin received an MFA in Advanced Photographic Studies from the International Center of Photography/ Bard College. Temkin’s experimentation with programming as a form of art, the discrepancies between algorithmic and human thought, and the fragmented patterns of thinking. Temkin’s ongoing project, Glitchometry, is a collaborative project between machine and man. Since there is a trade-off in control of creation of the work between the machine and the image manipulator, the resulting images are representations of the relationship between humans and machines where the compulsiveness that comes from humanity’s attempts to logically think and the desire for accessible forms of tumultuous processes clash.


Daniel Temkin's Glitchometry Stripes #4 (2013) and Glitchometry Stripes #4 Alternate (2013)

Temkin’s Glitchometry project inspires me to perform my own experiments on the collaboration between myself and machines with the creation of artworks.

LaTurbo Avedon is an avatar curator and artist who originates from virtual space and works on the internet. Avedon’s work emphasizes the concept of authorship and identity that is nonphysical. Avedon’s process of creating character is present in their performance, gaming, and exhibitions. Along with the exploration of the physicality of the internet and the idea of virtual authorship, Avedon also researches the explosions, deconstructions, and dimensions of forms. Avedon’s piece ID is a 2015 projection installation that deals with the identification of the digital self. The work is meant to address how social media applications have carried on towards the development of authenticators that add real name policies and the addition of greedy terms of service in order to gain access to more personal information from users.

LaTurbo Avedon's ID (2015)

Avedon as an artist caught my attention quickly, being an avatar that creates their own work. However, their work kept my interest because of its character based elements. Avedon’s existence and work are both explorations of the relationship between the real and virtual worlds which is part of what I want to do with my own work.

Therefore, Carla Gannis, Daniel Temkin, and LaTurbo Avedon are all artists that represent New Media Art. Each, in their own way, explores new techniques and ways to create art.


"Not a Y/N Fantasy"


"Not a Y/N Fantasy," 2020

This is an interactive story created with HTML, CSS, and some Bootstrap 4 that allows the reader to step into the role of the main character of a story. Many aspects of the story are based on y/n fanfiction tropes in order to poke a bit of lighthearted fun at the genre that often romanticizes certain famous characters and individuals. The interactivity of the story helps the reader engage more with the work and allows them to dive even deeper into the main concept of idealization and how people or situations may not always turn out the way one was hoping.

"Immortal Glitch"




"Immortal Glitch," 2020

With an image from one of my favorite movies called "What We Do in the Shadows," I used Audacity to create a glitched version. The final glitched image shows the effects of numerous different changes and saves. I chose to glitch this image because I wanted to portray how the variations of vampires within movies and fiction creates a blurred idea of the supernatural vampire identity.

"It Was a Couple of Days Ago"


"It Was a Couple of Days Ago," Digital Video, 3 m 51 s, 2020

The topic of this piece is manipulative editing and its ability to blur the lines between what is real and fake. The narrative of a video is dependent on its portrayal through the editing. Thus, the person or group in control of the video creation has a lot of power. Since video is a convenient and efficient way to get information out to a large amount of people, it can be easy to take advantage of its storytelling power to further one's personal agendas rather than convey the truth.

Featuring: Gabe Mendoza and Lily Mendoza


"Ticking"


"Ticking," Digital Video, 6 m 14 s, December 2020

This video explores the interrelationship between light and darkness in people regarding how they deal with emotions of annoyance, anger, and aggravation. The narrative presents the seemingly comical interactions between two siblings who do not get along very well, as their disdain for each other grows. Instead of communicating their issues with one another, they bottle up their anger until they explode. The video concludes ambiguously, leaving the viewer alone with their own interpretation. This piece contains some violence.

Featuring: Gabe Mendoza and Connie Mendoza

"Meet Yin and Yang"


"Meet Yin and Yang," Augmented Reality, November 2020

This is an interactive AR work that portrays the intertwined relationship between light and darkness. The scene parallels the two complementary forces through a combination of illustrations, GIFs, and 3D objects. "YinYang"



"YinYang," Virtual Digital Video Installation, 3 m 43 s, 2020

I collaborated with Lily Mendoza for "YinYang." This piece is inspired by the complementary relationship between light and darkness. While the videos initially separate the light from the darkness through parallels and juxtaposition, the narrative concludes with the presence of both forces in both worlds and characters. Thus, the work reveals the interrelation of both concepts which are a part of every person and the basis of the complexity of human nature and life itself. This collection of four short films are meant to be projected simultaneously on the four walls of a room. We wanted to immerse the viewer in the two realms that we created, so they can experience the light and dark closely without escape.

Featuring: Leksi Lincoln, Lanie Lincoln, and Gabe Mendoza

"Imagination"









"Imagination," Digital Photography, 2020.

This collection of photos portrays my interpretation of the word "imagination." Initially, I planned for the images to reflect the excitement, liveliness, and youthfulness associated with imagination. However, after photographing a few different scenes, I felt as if I had captured something else. I originally believed that an imaginative outlook involved seeing the world through bright, colorful lenses, but I realized that is not necessarily true. A person’s imagination can be dark as well. Sometimes an imaginative perspective paired with fear may cause someone to be afraid of something that isn’t even real. When I was a child, I was not necessarily as afraid of darkness as I was of what I thought may be lurking in it. Thus, there are numerous different aspects to a person’s imagination, and I tried to explore my own through these photos.
